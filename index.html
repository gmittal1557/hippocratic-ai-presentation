The problem

Most colonoscopies don't fail because of the procedure. They fail before the procedure ever starts.

Twenty to twenty-five percent of colonoscopies have inadequate bowel prep. About eight percent are cancelled or aborted same-day. At a high-volume GI service line doing 30,000 procedures a year, that's three million dollars or more in preventable annual revenue loss — plus the downstream cost of repeat procedures within 12 months.

But the cost isn't only financial. A colonoscopy that fails because of prep is a patient who took the day off work, drank a gallon of laxative, found a ride to the hospital — and now has to do it all again. Most of these failures are preventable.

Source: published cohort studies of GI endoscopy units.

Most centers' pre-procedure call happens 3–7 days before the procedure. The published RCT shows the call that actually moves prep adequacy is the one made the night before — but staffing it across a high-volume center would require approximately 9 dedicated evening nurses.
See the published evidence
  • Liu et al., 2021 — RCT showing Day -1 telephone education improved adequate prep from 70.3% to 81.6% (n=470)
  • Kohli et al., 2018 — multi-site retrospective cohort (n=4,016) showing scheduled Day -5 nurse calls had no measurable effect on prep adequacy
  • ASGE GI Operations Benchmarking Survey, 2019 — same-day cancellation rates of 5.6–8.45% across U.S. endoscopy units

Why an agent fixes it

The Day-before evening call is the highest-leverage intervention published in the literature. Hospitals haven't deployed it because it doesn't scale on human labor. A voice agent makes the unstaffable workflow possible.

What the agent does

A voice agent that calls every patient scheduled for colonoscopy at approximately 6 PM the evening before — coaches them through bowel prep, catches errors in real time, and escalates to a GI nurse when prep is failing.

What it does

  • Coaches bowel prep at the moment of action
  • Confirms perioperative medications
  • Triages symptoms; escalates to nurse on red flags
  • Confirms transportation and arrival logistics
  • Documents the call in Epic (note + flag + observations + In-Basket)

What it deliberately does not do

  • Schedule colonoscopies (Hippocratic's existing COL agent already does this)
  • Diagnose conditions or make clinical decisions
  • Address transportation barriers (Phase 2 — separate agent)
  • Handle financial counseling (Phase 2 — separate agent)
  • Replace the GI nurse — escalates to one when needed

How it integrates

Five components, four connections. Everything else is detail.

3 PM — pre-call pull

  • Appointment
  • Patient
  • ServiceRequest
  • Practitioner, Location
  • Condition (filtered)
  • MedicationStatement (filtered)
  • Observation (recent labs)
  • AllergyIntolerance
  • DocumentReference (prep templates)

6 PM — the call

  • Voice over Twilio SIP
  • Polaris LLM reasoning
  • Real-time turn-taking
  • Warm transfer on escalation
  • Pre-loaded data drives conversation; no live FHIR calls during the call

6:05 PM — write-back

  • DocumentReference (structured note)
  • Communication (call disposition)
  • Flag (if risk identified)
  • Observation (patient-reported data)
  • In-Basket message (if escalation needed)

All reads happen at 3 PM. Pre-loaded data drives the call. Writes fire after the call ends, within seconds.

FHIR-first, hybrid in reality.

The primary path is FHIR R4 for reads and most writes. HL7 v2 fallback for sites not yet FHIR-current. Epic-specific operations such as Communication.$send-in-basket for In-Basket messaging where standard FHIR doesn't cover the workflow.

Pre-load discipline.

The agent doesn't fetch data live during conversations. Comprehensive pre-load at 3 PM means zero EHR-dependent latency during patient calls. Voice doesn't tolerate the silence of a mid-conversation tool call. This is a deliberate safety and UX choice.

The hardest deployment problem.

Bowel prep regimen choice doesn't live in a standard FHIR field. It's a 30-minute working session per customer in deployment week one. Not a code problem — a configuration problem.

Tradeoffs and roadmap

Three things will be hard. Here's how I'd handle each.

Risk 1: GI nurse adoption.

Nurses worry the agent will replace them or that it will generate too many false escalations and waste their time. A failed adoption looks like nurses ignoring agent flags or routing patients around the agent.

Mitigation

Position the agent as a triage layer that increases nurse leverage — nurses get higher-quality patients to review, not more patients. Track escalation precision (percent of escalated calls where the nurse actually intervened) as a primary metric. Co-design escalation rules with the GI service line in deployment week one. Expect the first month of production to require active tuning.

Risk 2: Patient pushback on robocalls.

Patients hate cold-call IVRs and may hang up before the agent can establish what it is. Older patients in particular may distrust the medium.

Mitigation

Caller ID branding (the call shows "Cleveland Clinic GI" not a random number). Opt-in at scheduling so patients are pre-warned. Evening time window respected (no calls before 5 PM or after 9 PM). Immediate handoff to a human within 30 seconds when patient asks. Hippocratic's published 9.0/10 patient ratings on existing post-discharge agents are evidence the approach works when these principles are followed.

Risk 3: Multi-language deployment.

A high-volume AMC serves Spanish, Mandarin, Vietnamese, and other-language patients. Day-one deployment in English only would exclude the populations who most need attention.

Mitigation

Day-one deployment includes English and Spanish. Phase two expands per customer demand. Important not to over-promise on day one — patients don't tolerate a robocall in the wrong language. Better to escalate to a human nurse for non-supported languages than to attempt a poor agent experience.

This agent addresses the prep-related share of colonoscopy misses — about 30% of the failure modes. Other failure types (transportation, fear of findings, financial access) each deserve their own agent with different conversational depth, different integrations, and different safety architecture. The provider deployment generates the outcome data that makes the payor expansion fast.

Why provider-first, payor-second

Payors have a real and growing interest in colonoscopy outcomes. The new HEDIS COL-FU measure tracks completion of follow-up colonoscopy after positive stool tests — currently sitting around 35–65% completion. Federal regulations as of January 2023 eliminated patient cost-sharing for these follow-up procedures, which puts payors more financially on the hook for completion.

A Medicare Advantage plan with 200,000 members in the screening-age population sees roughly 20,000 colonoscopies per year. If our agent reduces inadequate prep rate by 8 percentage points, that's 1,600 fewer repeat procedures within 12 months — about $2.4M in avoided medical spend.

But the right sequence is provider deployment first. Providers feel the pain immediately through cancelled OR slots. Payors feel it annually through HEDIS attestation. The provider deployment generates per-call outcome data that makes the payor sale fast and defensible. Going to a payor first means making claims about effect size we can't yet prove.

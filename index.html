01
The Problem

1 in 5 colonoscopies has inadequate prep.

  • — 20 to 25% of colonoscopies have inadequate bowel prep
  • — 8% are cancelled or aborted same day
  • — $3M+ in preventable annual revenue per high-volume gastroenterology (GI) service line
  • — Patients still pay the cost. A day off work, a gallon of laxative, and a repeat visit.

Day-1 evening calls work. But staffing them at scale would cost 9 dedicated evening nurses, a position no GI service line wants to add.
  • Liu et al., 2021 — RCT showing Day -1 telephone education improved adequate prep from 70.3% to 81.6% (n=470)
  • Kohli et al., 2018 — multi-site retrospective cohort (n=4,016) showing scheduled Day -5 nurse calls had no measurable effect on prep adequacy
  • ASGE GI Operations Benchmarking Survey, 2019 — same-day cancellation rates of 5.6–8.45% across U.S. endoscopy units

What it does

  • Coaches prep, confirms meds, triages symptoms
  • Confirms logistics: nothing by mouth, escort, arrival
  • Writes back to Epic: note, Flag, Observation, In-Basket

What it deliberately does not do

  • Make clinical decisions or interpret labs
  • Address transport or financial barriers (Phase 2 agents)
  • Replace the GI nurse; escalates to one when criteria are met

Configuration

How it's configured

Service-type codes
Your local codes for colonoscopy. Varies per site. Could be SNOMED, Epic-internal, or local.
In-Basket pool routing
Which nurse pool gets escalations. Different sites route differently.
Note type for write-back
Which Epic note type our structured notes file under. Must match an existing template the GI nurses already review.
Escalation triggers
Site-specific risk thresholds. Vomiting count, prep timing slippage, missing escort.
Prep regimens by physician
Each GI doc has standing preferences. Dr. Smith uses split-dose SUPREP. Dr. Patel uses MoviPrep. Captured in deployment week one.
Perioperative protocol per physician
Anticoagulant hold rules, insulin adjustments. Per-site, sometimes per-physician within a site.

Four design choices.

01

Pre-load, then verify.

All FHIR reads happen at 3 PM. The agent verbally re-confirms critical items — meds, escort — during the call. Chart drives the questions; patient answers drive the flags.

02

Read minimum-necessary, not everything.

Filter Condition and MedicationRequest to fields relevant to this procedure. PHI exposure scales with what you read.

03

Customer-authorized service.

IT approves it once. Read access to seven FHIR resources, write to four. Scoped to the GI service line.

04

When the pre-load fails.

If a patient's chart can't be loaded at 3 PM: skip that call, alert the nurse pool, trigger the customer's existing SMS reminder.

05
Deployment

Open deployment questions.

What I'd resolve with the customer before going live.

01 Week 1

Who owns escalation routing?

The agent can detect a deviation and decide to escalate — but to whom, through what channel, and with what SLA? That decision belongs to the GI service line, not engineering. We need a named nurse or coordinator, an on-call fallback, and a documented response window before the first live call.

02 Week 1

What is the right escalation threshold?

A threshold set too low floods nurses with low-acuity flags and kills adoption. Too high and we miss real prep failures. The right setting depends on the nurse team's capacity and risk tolerance — not on a default we ship. I'd spend deployment week one pairing with the clinical team to calibrate this on historical data before going live.

03 Week 2

How do we measure success?

Colonoscopy prep adequacy rate is the outcome that matters, but it takes weeks to accumulate. Leading indicators — call completion rate, escalation precision, patient-reported confidence score — let us tune the agent before the lagging metric arrives. We need agreement on the primary metric and a dashboard owner before launch, not after.

06
Roadmap

Success and next steps.

What we measure, and what we build next.

How we'd know it's working.

Did it work?

  • Did patients arrive with clean preps? Bowel-prep quality scored at the procedure.
  • Did fewer procedures get cancelled day-of? Same-day cancellation rate vs. baseline.

Is the system running?

  • Are we reaching patients? Connection rate on outbound calls.
  • Are they staying on the line? Completion rate among connected calls.

Is it safe?

  • Are nurses correcting the agent's notes? Override rate as a proxy for accuracy.
  • Any patient safety incidents? Adverse-event count. Target: zero.

What we'd build next.

01 Months 3–6

Other GI prep procedures.

EGD prep, capsule endoscopy prep. Same patient population, same FHIR integration. Fastest extension of the pilot.

02 Months 6–9

Day-after follow-up call.

Same patient, 24 hours later. Confirms recovery, captures symptoms, surfaces escalations. Closes the loop on the procedure.

03 Months 9–12

Follow-up after positive at-home stool test.

Different trigger — patient's at-home stool test came back positive and they need a diagnostic colonoscopy. Same site, same handoff pattern.
